The realization that we do not live in a world of “literal things,” but rather a web of metaphors, is often described by cognitive scientists as the “Aha!” moment of human consciousness. To discover that metaphors are not just poetic flourishes but the very scaffolding of our reality is to look behind the curtain of the human mind.

The Architecture of the Unseen: How Metaphor Constructs Reality

  1. The Linguistic Foundation: We Live By Them For centuries, metaphors were viewed merely as “fancy talk”—ornaments used by poets to dress up plain facts. However, in 1980, linguists George Lakoff and Mark Johnson revolutionized our understanding with Metaphors We Live By. They argued that our conceptual system is fundamentally metaphorical.

Consider the common metaphor: “Argument is War.”

We attack every weak point.

We defend our positions.

We win or lose the debate.

Because we view arguments through the lens of battle, we behave aggressively. If we viewed “Argument as a Dance,” the goal would not be to defeat the other person, but to perform a balanced, aesthetic movement together. Our metaphors don’t just describe our behavior; they command it.

  1. The Mathematical Surprise: Numbers in Space It is a common misconception that mathematics is the realm of “pure, literal truth” devoid of human bias. In reality, mathematics is built upon grounded metaphors.

Cognitive science suggests that humans understand abstract math through our physical experience in the world:

The Number Line: We conceptualize numbers as points on a path. We “count up” or “count down,” treating numbers as if they were physical locations.

Sets as Containers: When we talk about a number being “in” a set, we are using the physical experience of putting an object inside a box.

Arithmetic as Motion: Adding is “moving forward”; subtracting is “moving back.”

Without these spatial metaphors, our brains would have no physical “hook” to hang abstract variables on. Even the most complex equations in physics are, at their heart, metaphors for how we perceive movement and force in the 3D world.

  1. The Philosophical Mirror: Nietzsche’s “Mobile Army” The philosopher Friedrich Nietzsche took this discovery to its furthest limit. He argued that “truth” itself is nothing more than a “mobile army of metaphors, metonyms, and anthropomorphisms.”

According to Nietzsche, humans take a nerve stimulus and turn it into an image (the first metaphor), then turn that image into a sound or word (the second metaphor). Eventually, we forget these are metaphors and begin to call them “Truths.” This “big surprise” you felt is essentially the moment you realized that what we call “reality” is actually a long-standing, collective poem that we have forgotten is a poem.

The Cultural Synthesis Every culture builds its world on different metaphorical pillars:

Time: In the West, “Time is Money” (we spend it, waste it, save it). In many Indigenous cultures, “Time is a River” or “Time is a Circle,” leading to a completely different relationship with productivity and patience.

The Self: We often say, “I’m falling apart” or “I need to pull myself together,” treating the human psyche as a physical object that can break or be mended.

Summary of the “Metaphorical Mind” Field Primary Metaphorical Function Language To categorize abstract experiences into relatable physical actions. Mathematics To map abstract logic onto our innate sense of space and motion. Philosophy To create a “shorthand” for reality so we can navigate existence. Behavior To provide a “script” for social interaction (e.g., life is a journey).

Conclusion: The Grand Revelation The “astonishing surprise” you experienced is the discovery of the Cognitive Map. Once you see that “High Status” is “Up,” that “Affection” is “Warmth,” and that “The Future” is “In Front,” you can never un-see it. You realize that humanity is not just a biological species, but a symbolic one. We don’t just eat, sleep, and breathe; we interpret, we map, and we imagine.

We are the only creatures on Earth who can look at a flickering light and see “hope,” or look at a mathematical equation and see “the mind of God.”
